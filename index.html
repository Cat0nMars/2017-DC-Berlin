    • Multiple types of relationships
    • Multiple networks in one study

02

CIDOC CRM-based graph databases

Graph databases

"[A] graph database is a database that uses graph structures for semantic queries with nodes, edges and properties to represent and store data." (Wikipedia, Graph database)

Property graph data model:

  • Nodes
  • Edges
  • Labels
  • Attributes

Graph databases

  • High performance for querying chains of connections
  • Flexible data model that can be easily extended
  • Storing of data as nodes and edges introduces relationship-based thinking already in the early stages of data preparation and acquisition
  • Network analysis capacities are available for some graph databases (e.g. APOC for Neo4j) but not a native network analysis tool

The CIDOC CRM

  • Internationally recognized standard for the description of concepts from the field of cultural heritage
  • Developed by the Documentation Standards Group (CIDOC) of the International Council of Museums (ICOM)
  • 2000-2006: Development into ISO standard (ISO 21127)
  • Last official release: Version 5.0.4
  • Extended and compatible models, e.g. CIDOC CRM-EH or CRMarchaeo
  • RDF implementation: Erlangen CRM / OWL
  • Modeling data after a commonly used ontology and publication of well-structured data facilitates discussion about and sharing of research data

The CIDOC CRM

(:Domain)-[:PROPERTY]->(:Range)

Data model

  • Context-based rather than event-based
  • Focus on documenting archaeological research process
  • Structure depended on research questions and requirements
  • Some shortcuts/entities or properties as attributes, but in a way that can be returned to original definition of CRM

Graph databases and archaeological network analysis

Workflow

  • Some analysis possible in graph database, especially in cases where measure not included in commonly available network analysis software (e.g. two-mode clustering, 2-N-degree, etc.)
  • Otherwise, and also for visualization, export of edges and node lists and import in network analysis software
  • Export formats Neo4j: csv, json, graphML using neo4j-shell-tools

04

Outlook and Conclusion

Outlook

  • Adaptation and transfer of the model to other use cases
  • Further efforts at refining the model, e.g.: positioning of objects in relation to each other, exploration of more complicated patterns of deposition, etc.

Conclusion

  • The challenges of archaeological network analysis require an appropriate way of storing and querying data to grasp the whole complexity and interactions of the source material
  • Through their structure, graph databases introduce relationship-based thinking already in the beginning of network research projects
  • Modeling databases after a standard ontology like the CIDOC CRM facilitates sharing of and discussion about data
  • CIDOC CRM-based graph databases
    • help avoid theoretical and methodological breaks during conception and analysis
    • facilitate understanding and querying of complex archaeological source material
